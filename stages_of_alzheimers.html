Disease Progression Guide

The Stages of Alzheimer‚Äôs

While every journey is unique, understanding the typical progression helps families plan, adapt, and provide the best care at every turn.

Stage 1

Preclinical (No Symptoms)

Brain changes begin years before any noticeable signs. During this silent phase, amyloid plaques and tangles start to form, but the individual appears perfectly healthy.

No outward cognitive decline
Identifiable only through biomarkers/imaging
Can last for decades
Stage 2

Mild (Early-Stage)

Subtle difficulties emerge that might be mistaken for "normal aging." The individual can often still live independently but may struggle with complex tasks.

Forgetting recent conversations
Difficulty finding the right words
Misplacing valuable objects
Trouble planning or organizing
Stage 3

Moderate (Middle-Stage)

This is typically the longest stage. Symptoms become pronounced, and the need for daily assistance grows as confusion and personality changes set in.

Confusion about time and place
Difficulty recognizing friends/family
Personality changes (agitation, apathy)
Needing help with dressing/bathing
Stage 4

Severe (Late-Stage)

In the final stage, cognitive and physical abilities decline significantly. Around-the-clock care is required as the brain loses its ability to control the body.

Loss of ability to communicate
Profound memory loss (unaware of surroundings)
Physical decline (walking, swallowing)
Total dependence on caregivers

Early Detection is Key

If you notice these signs in yourself or a loved one, consult a healthcare professional. Early diagnosis allows for better symptoms management and future planning.
